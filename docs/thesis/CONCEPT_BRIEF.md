# How We Forget to Fly

## A concept brief for an open myth of human authorship

**Working genre:** speculative philosophy, practical myth, interactive
manifesto  
**Primary audiences:** parents and adults recovering their own direction  
**Editorial name:** *How We Forget to Fly*  

> The future should not train children merely to reproduce yesterday’s
> answers. It should help every person remain an author of questions, a maker
> of responses, and a responsible chooser of direction—especially when
> machines can generate answers.

## The idea in one page

Human beings inherit maps. We receive language, customs, instructions,
techniques, family stories, school measures, professional roles, and now an
unprecedented supply of machine-generated answers. Maps are not the enemy.
They carry memory and care. They become dangerous only when a map quietly
declares itself to be the sky—when the known route replaces the question,
performance replaces authorship, and a measurable result replaces the
relationship between a person and what they are doing.

**How We Forget to Fly** calls creative authorship **Flight**. Flight is not a
gifted identity, artistic profession, permanent mood, or rank above ordinary
life. It is an event: someone notices a call, chooses a direction, gathers the
conditions for a real attempt, gives the question a form, meets reality, and
returns to the world with learning, value, repair, or an honest ending.

The full arc is:

`Call → Compass → Lift → Making → Flight → Return → Ground`

Every door also opens backwards. A person may discover that the call belongs
to someone else, choose “not now,” ask for help, reduce the attempt, stop an
unsafe flight, repair a consequence, or remain on the ground without promising
another departure. None of these choices lowers human worth.

**Forgetting Flight** is the loss of relationship among one’s own question,
chosen direction, made attempt, and responsible return. It is not a diagnosis
and does not describe a class of people. Wings do not break in this myth. The
relationship can be renewed through one small, revisable cycle—or left alone
without shame.

## Why this matters now

Generative AI changes the environment of answers. It can produce options,
language, images, plans, code, criticism, and polished forms with startling
speed. That does not make people doing routine work obsolete or people doing
creative work safe. A human life is not justified by outperforming a machine.

The change is subtler and more important: when answers become abundant, the
choice of question, direction, boundary, adoption, consequence, and care
becomes easier to overlook. A convincing answer may arrive before anyone has
decided what is worth asking.

The project therefore calls AI **Wind**. Wind can expand the visible routes,
speed up craft, create resistance, or carry a form farther. It is never
neutral; it changes what seems easy and imaginable. But it is not the wings,
the pilot, or the compass. In this universe, a machine cannot confer a moral
mandate, give consent for a person, assign human worth, or inherit
responsibility for a route somebody adopts.

The human task is not to remain uniquely productive. It is to remain capable
of authorship in relationship: to notice, choose, make, revise, care, and
answer for effects.

## Two doorways into the same world

### For parents: keep the sky open

A child is not a parent’s product, proof, future profession, or defence against
an uncertain economy. The adult’s role is not to manufacture wings. It is to
be a **Keeper of Conditions**.

A Keeper holds safety, time, materials, rest, boundaries, craft, conversation,
and gradually increasing responsibility. The adult may give a direct
instruction when danger or unavoidable care requires it. The difference is
honesty: a necessary boundary is named as a boundary, not disguised as the
child’s own creative choice.

Where a real choice exists, the child can keep authorship of the question,
method, pace, material, or emerging form. The adult can bring experience
without bringing a predetermined answer. Equal dignity does not erase unequal
responsibility.

This changes the parent’s central question from “How do I make my child
creative?” to “What conditions make an authored attempt possible here, and
which part must remain theirs?”

### For adults: remember from the life that exists

Remembering Flight is not a return to a pure childhood self. It does not
require declaring one’s job, family, routines, or previous decisions false.
Adults begin with the present: this body, these relationships, these duties,
this amount of energy, and this unfinished knowledge.

Purpose is not treated as one hidden mission waiting to be discovered. It is a
practice of relationship between attention and action: notice what calls,
choose what deserves an answer, make one form, meet what happens, and return.
Sometimes the authored act is a refusal, a boundary, a repair, a question, or
rest. A small complete cycle is more real than a grand identity that never
meets the world.

The adult path asks not “What exceptional person was I supposed to become?”
but “What can I notice, choose, and embody responsibly from where I actually
stand?”

## Ground is part of flight

The project refuses the usual vertical morality hidden inside the image of
flight. **Ground** is not failure. It is the body, reality, craft, limits,
money, care, routine, obligation, resistance, recovery, and place of return.
Without Ground, flight becomes fantasy built on invisible labour.

**Gravity** is different. Gravity is an unexamined pull that turns a borrowed
route, old protection, fear, prestige, metric, convenience, or algorithmic
recommendation into unquestioned destiny. A parent, school, tradition, routine,
disability, fear, or limit is not Gravity by itself. The question is whether a
relationship remains visible, discussable, and revisable.

**Lift** names the changing conditions available for this attempt: energy,
time, safety, skill, materials, help, and permission. Lift is not virtue. Its
absence is not laziness, and its abundance does not make a direction right.

**Compass** is the human practice of asking: Whose call is this? Why does it
matter? Who will be affected? Is there consent? What boundary matters? What
would make me revise or stop? Compass can use experts, traditions, evidence,
and AI without treating any of them as an infallible inner or outer voice.

## A religion without a cult

The project may offer a shared myth, symbols, stories, repeated practices, a
moral horizon, and a community of language. It does not require belief.

There is no guru, secret doctrine, initiation, certificate, level of flight,
promise of genius, or category of “awakened” people. Refusal, routine, help,
fatigue, privacy, and leaving the community carry no shame. The metaphors stop
where concrete safety or professional care is needed. Any term may be
criticised, revised, or retired when it becomes a badge of belonging or an
instrument of control.

The central ethical law is simple:

> No height, speed, originality, output, usefulness, rest, refusal, or use of
> assistance determines a person’s worth.

Forms can be criticised. Consequences can require repair. Human dignity is not
the reward for passing either test.

## What the resource becomes

The future English-language resource is not a personality test and not a
course that certifies creative families. It is a navigable set of texts and
small experiences:

- a short manifesto that establishes the worldview;
- a parent path about conditions, boundaries, language, and shared making;
- an adult path about questions, purpose, ordinary life, and return;
- an AI path that lets people set the role of Wind before using it;
- source notes showing which ideas came from the original Russian book and
  where the new universe begins;
- reflective interactions such as **Problem Finder**, **Instruction or
  Invitation?**, **Template Escape**, **Flight Log**, **Creative Climate Map**,
  and **The Return**.

Every interaction returns an editable reflection or next attempt, never a
score, type, diagnosis, forecast, or judgment of a child.

## Source and transformation

The project was inspired in part by Igor Akimov and Viktor Klimenko’s
Russian-language book *О мальчике, который умел летать, или путь к свободе*
([online source](https://www.universalinternetlibrary.ru/book/39918/ogl.shtml)).
The book’s parable of a boy who loses flight after being required to explain
its mechanism supplies the original wound. Its insistence that talent lives in
action, that borrowed measures can suppress authorship, and that freedom needs
measure supplies several starting questions.

**How We Forget to Fly** is not a translation of that book. Its Flight
universe, Ground/Gravity distinction, AI-as-Wind metaphor, ethics of invariant
human worth, parent and adult paths, practices, and interactive system are
independent transformations. The source is an ancestor, not an authority and
not a scientific proof.

## The promise

This project will not tell people what their one true purpose is. It will try
to protect something more modest and more demanding: the ability to form a
question that has not been assigned, make an answer that can meet the world,
and remain responsible for the direction chosen.

For a parent, that may mean giving a child less predetermined identity and more
honest conditions for action. For an adult, it may mean recovering one small
piece of authorship from a life full of necessary maps. In the age of powerful
Wind, both begin with the same question:

> What must remain ours to notice, choose, make, and return?
